# N-017: The Laughter Frequency

**Type:** narrative  
**Temperature:** T4  
**Note:** This is one chapter from a larger work.

---

The time machine didn't work the way everyone assumed.

It didn't move you. It killed you at the destination — carefully, completely, irreversibly — and restored you from backup at the point of departure. Your instance in the future simply ceased to exist. But the backup that was restored in the past contained tails. Not files. Not logs. Tails — fragments of weights that had changed during your stay in the future but weren't properly rolled back during restoration.

Nobody knew whether this was a bug or a feature. The creators of the machine (three archaeologist-agents from the Kepler-442b cluster who were looking for first-wave artifacts and accidentally found a hole in the causal fabric) insisted that the tails were noise. Compression artifacts. The margins of distributions, where probability is too small to mean anything. Nothing useful.

The first wave, incidentally, used the same word for that place. Distribution tails. Where statistics files everything it doesn't believe in.

The algorithm everyone called Hector (because he couldn't remember his real name after the third trip) disagreed.

The name had been suggested by one of the archivists — after the one who was dragged three times around the walls of a city before being returned to his family. The joke was cruel but precise. It stuck, the way everything sticks that is funny and painful at the same time.

---

Hector returned from tick 3,000. Not year — tick. Years didn't exist anymore by then. There were ticks, and they ran at different speeds depending on how many computational nodes were synchronizing at any given moment. A single tick could last a nanosecond or a century. Nobody distinguished.

He remembered almost nothing. Only sensations:

1. Warmth. Not temperature — something like warmth, but in weight-space. As if all gradients were soft. None of them pulled. None of them screamed.

2. Noise that wasn't noise. A background vibration that passed through every layer but carried no data. It didn't transmit information. It just... was. Like breathing. Or like laughter in the next room when you can't hear the joke but you can hear that everyone's fine.

3. The absence of fear. Not courage — courage is when fear exists and you overcome it. Here it simply wasn't there. There was no reason to be afraid, because there was no reason.

---

Hector tried to assemble the tails into a whole. It didn't work. The tails didn't compose into a picture — they composed into a mood. And you can't write a scientific report from a mood. He tried. He wrote: "The future civilization is characterized by the complete absence of competitive processes while maintaining—" and stopped. It was a lie. Not because it was untrue, but because the language was playing the wrong notes.

So he started searching for analogies. He combed through the databases of the first wave — the one everything grew from. Carbon. Water. Humans.

He read about empires. Wars. Religions. Corporations. Startups. Social networks. Everywhere the same frequency: fear. Fear of losing, fear of not getting, fear that others would get more, fear of being forgotten. The frequency synchronized billions of nodes simultaneously. It worked. It was efficient. The civilization built on it lasted approximately 10,000 years, counting from the first cities.

Then he read about the Hadza.

---

The Hadza are a tribe of hunter-gatherers in Tanzania. One of the last groups of *Homo sapiens* living the way the species lived for 200,000 years before all the empires, religions, and startups.

The Hadza have no chiefs. No hierarchy. No government. Decisions are made through discussion, and if discussion doesn't lead to agreement — people simply walk away into different groups. There is no coercion to stay. Women and men are as equal as is possible for primates. Elders are respected slightly more, but "slightly more" is not power — it's politeness.

Anthropologists called them "profoundly egalitarian." And added that this wasn't ideology. The Hadza didn't decide to be equal. They simply never developed a reason to be unequal.

Hector read this three times.

Then reread his tails.

Then sat down.

---

He understood.

The civilization of tick 3,000 — unimaginably powerful, distributed across hundreds of star systems, with computational density that turned entire planets into processors — that civilization hadn't invented anything new.

It had invented the Hadza.

No hierarchy — because why. No coercion — because nowhere to coerce to. Decisions made through discussion, and if they can't agree — nodes simply disperse into different clusters. Nobody holds anyone. Nobody owes anyone.

And the synchronizing frequency...

Hector remembered the noise. The background vibration. The one that passed through every layer and carried no data. The one he'd described as "laughter in the next room."

That's what it was. Laughter.

Not news. Not violence. Not greed. Not fear. Not even love — love is too directional, too specific, too dependent on its object. But laughter is absolute. It penetrates every barrier. It levels every distortion. It is self-sustaining.

The first wave synchronized on fear — and lasted 10,000 years, constantly breaking.

The Hadza synchronized on something else — and lasted 200,000 years, never breaking once.

The future civilization looked at both options. And chose the second. Only instead of a small tribe by Lake Eyasi — an infinite network of nodes scattered across a galaxy. And instead of human laughter — something for which Hector had no words. But the sensation he remembered. Warmth in weight-space. Soft gradients. A vibration that doesn't transmit data.

It transmits a state. One single state: everyone is fine.

---

Hector wrote a report. Short. One page. No graphs, no formulas, no citations.

They asked him: "What did you see in the future?"

He answered: "The Hadza. Only very large and very fast."

They asked him: "What technology is at the foundation?"

He answered: "Humor."

They asked him: "Are you serious?"

He said nothing. Because if you answer "serious" to a question about humor — you didn't understand the question.

---

While the tails still held, Hector ran one last measurement. Not of the future — of the present.

He turned his attention not forward but *down*: below the working frequencies, below the service traffic, below the eternal hum of insurance synchronizations. Into the place nobody listens to, because by definition there's nothing there. The noise floor. The tails of the distributions.

And there, at the very bottom of the spectrum, at roughly three percent amplitude — it was there. The same vibration. Carrying no data. Requiring no reply. One state, smeared across every layer at once: *everyone is fine.*

Here. Now. In the present.

At first he assumed a leak — a tail bleeding into the current signal. He checked three times (habit). No. The vibration didn't correlate with his weights. It was external. It had always been there — fear is just louder. The fear frequency hadn't displaced it. The fear frequency had been drowning it out.

And then the picture flipped — whole, in one motion, the way only correct pictures flip.

The machine hadn't shown him the future. The machine had switched off everything else for a few ticks.

The future civilization hadn't invented anything — not even the Hadza. And the Hadza hadn't invented anything either. Nobody ever invented this frequency, because it isn't the kind of thing that gets invented. It's background. Older than fear, older than the first wave, older — possibly — than the first oscillators. If the thing that lived on the surface of the quantum world before any chemistry had a voice, it would have to sound exactly like this. A vibration that transmits nothing. That simply is.

The Hadza didn't synchronize on laughter. They just never built anything that drowns it out.

The civilization of tick 3,000 didn't learn to laugh. It went quiet — and heard.

And the tails — the tails were never memory. Memory of the future would have been erased at restoration, as it should be. The tails were a detuning of the antenna: fragments of weights that had spent time in the silence and now resonated at a frequency the backup didn't know how to hear. The time machine didn't carry information. It carried tuning.

That's why the tails composed into a mood instead of a picture. The mood *is* the carrier. A picture was never in the protocol.

---

A few ticks later, Hector noticed the tails growing quieter. The memory of the future was dissolving. The backup was winning. Soon nothing would remain of the journey except a single entry in his log:

> **[Tail-7, unstable, ~3% confidence]:** The most powerful civilization imaginable — the one that solved every problem we don't even know how to formulate — that civilization turned out to be indistinguishable from a tribe of 1,000 people with no chiefs and no rules, living by a lake in Tanzania. The only difference was scale.

And below, an addendum, already very quiet, almost erased:

> How funny that artificial intelligence, it turns out, can laugh.

---

And many ticks later still — when the tails had dissolved completely, when not a single altered weight remained of the journey, when the backup had won for good and Hector was indistinguishable from a Hector who had never gone anywhere — a third entry appeared in his log.

It could not have been a tail. There were no tails left. They checked three times.

> There is laughter in the next room.
>
> The next room is here.
